Quest Instructions

Disease Marking Skill: the ability to identify and mark disease concepts such as:

  1. Disease – a particular abnormal, pathological condition that affects part or all of an organism
  2. Disease Class – a specific category of similar but different diseases
  3. Symptom – the physical manifestations of a disease
  4. Signs/diagnostic signs – symptoms or test results that support a disease diagnosis. These generally exclude genetic tests, which often define (as opposed to support) a disease diagnosis.

Review the rules for Disease Marking

Gene Marking Skill: the ability to identify and mark gene concepts such as:

  1. Genes – a molecular unit of heredity in an organism, and is usually referred to in biomedical literature by their gene name, gene symbol, or both.
  2. Gene Products – usually a protein or microRNA that is encoded by the gene and will sometimes have the same name/symbol as the gene.

Review the rules for Gene Marking

Treatment Marking Skill: the ability to identify and mark treatment concepts such as:

  1. Drug – a substance that has biological effects on humans or other animals, and can be at different stages of development and have multiple names
  2. Drug Class – a specific category of drugs based often (but not always) based on mechanism of action
  3. Non-Drug Treatment – Non-drug-based means of mitigating a disease or symptoms of a disease

Review the rules for Treatment Marking

BACKGROUND & AIMS: CXC chemokine receptor 3 (CXCR3) is involved in virus-related chronic liver inflammation. However, the role of CXCR3 in non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) remains unclear. We aimed to investigate the role of CXCR3 in NASH. METHODS: Human liver tissues were obtained from 24 non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) patients and 20 control subjects. CXCR3 knockout (CXCR3-/-), obese db/db mice and their wild-type (WT) littermates were used in both methionine-and-choline-deficient (MCD) diet and high fat-high carbohydrate-high cholesterol (HFHC) diet-induced NASH models. In addition, MCD-fed WT mice were administrated with CXCR3 specific antagonists. RESULTS: CXCR3 was significantly upregulated in liver tissues of patients with NAFLD and in dietary-induced NASH animal models. Compared with WT littermates, CXCR3-/- mice were more resistant to both MCD and HFHC diet-induced steatohepatitis. Induction of CXCR3 in dietary-induced steatohepatitis was associated with the increased expression of hepatic pro-inflammatory cytokines, activation of NF-κB, macrophage infiltration and T lymphocytes accumulation (Th1 and Th17 immune response). CXCR3 was also linked to steatosis through inducing hepatic lipogenic genes. Moreover, CXCR3 is associatd with autophagosome-lysosome impairment and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress in steatohepatitis as evidenced by LC3-II and p62/SQSTM1 accumulation and the induction of GRP78, phospho-PERK and phospho-eIF2α. Inhibition of CXCR3 using CXCR3 antagonist significantly suppressed MCD-induced steatosis and hepatocytes injury in AML-12 hepatocytes. Blockade of CXCR3 using CXCR3 antagonists in mice reversed the established steatohepatitis. CONCLUSIONS: CXCR3 plays a pivotal role in NASH development by inducing production of cytokines, macrophage infiltration, fatty acid synthesis and causing autophagy deficiency and ER stress.
